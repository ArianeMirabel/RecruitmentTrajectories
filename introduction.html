1 Introduction

Recruitment dynamics are by definition the main determinent of forest recovery following disturbance. These dynamics determine the vegetative structure of the forests, the ecosystem physiology, the carbon and nutrients cycling and the maintenance of species diversity (Anderson-Teixeira et al. 2013). Recruitment is the driving mechanisms of the Intermediate Disturbance Hypothesis (IDH) which explains the maintenance of tropical forests high biodiversity through the variability of environmental conditions, favoring the growth of a variety of species and thus the diversity of recruited trees . This makes recruitment dynamics the drivers of major dynamics, such as the tree fall gaps dynamics which continuously impulses different recruitment regime between enlightened, dry gaps and shaded, wet undercover areas.

Forests population dynamics were the focus of a large literature which successfully modeled the response of key forest features and functions such as carbon sequestration, commercial stocks, and diameter distribution dynamics in the short and in the long term . However, the response of communities on their whole and the underlying assembly rules remains unknown. Insights lready highlighted a combination of environmental filters and stochastic events as drivers of the response to disturbance, but the importance of recruitment dynamics have scarcely been disentangled. This hampers to have a refined understanding of response dynamics and to accurately forecast of future forests dynamics. If the regeneration of some species is successfully modeled, the response of the whole recruited community after disturbance but this approach requires an accurate knowledge of species ecology and behavior. It would not be workable for the community on its whole,given the number of species in tropical forests and the variability of growth and survival in the early life stage among species.

In this paper we aimed to have some insights into the role of demographic dynamics underlying the response of tropical forest after disturbance. From the 30 years survey of a tropical forest after disturbance following a gradient of disturbance intensity, we aimed to (i) identify the evolution of the taxonomic and functional diversity and the functional characteristics of commnities after disturbance, (ii) highlight the environmental filters ruling the recruitment according to the disturbance intensity, and (iii) highlight the recovery of communities and the existence of cyclic dynamics for the taxonomic and functional diversity.

  • Hypotheses:

  1. The more intense the logging, the most significant the impact on composition and diversity metrics.

  2. We expect an increase in the stand diversity (Intermediate disturbance hypothesis) or no change in richness, at least for low logging intensity

  3. Exploitation would change the functional composition toward more light-demanding strategies

  4. We expect the diversity to follow a cyclic dynamic, as observed for stands biomass, and the ecosystem functioning to be resilient, at least for low intensity treatment
